Jordan's Lemma

Source: Standard complex-analysis result; treatment follows J. E. Santos' Cambridge IB lectures.

Statement. Let $f(z) = e^{iaz}g(z)$ with $a > 0$. Show that if $g(z)\to 0$ uniformly on the upper half-plane as $|z|\to\infty$, i.e. \[\max_{\theta\in[0,\pi]}|g(Re^{i\theta})| \to 0 \quad \text{as } R\to\infty, \tag{1.1}\] then the integral over the upper semicircular arc $\mathcal{C}_R = \{z = Re^{i\theta},\, \theta\in[0,\pi]\}$ vanishes: \[\lim_{R\to\infty}\int_{\mathcal{C}_R}f(z)\,dz = 0.\tag{1.2}\] Find an upper bound for $\left|\int_{\mathcal{C}_R}f(z)\,dz\right|$ and give an example of a class of functions $g$ satisfying (1.1).

Solution A typical example satisfying (1.1) is any rational function $g(z) = P(z)/Q(z)$ with $\deg Q \geq \deg P + 1$, for which $\max|g(Re^{i\theta})| = \mathcal{O}(R^{-1})\to 0$.

Parametrise $z = Re^{i\theta}$, so $dz = iRe^{i\theta}d\theta$. The integral becomes \[\int_{\mathcal{C}_R}f(z)\,dz = iR\int^\pi_0 g(Re^{i\theta})\,e^{iaRe^{i\theta}}\,e^{i\theta}\,d\theta.\tag{1.3}\] Taking the absolute value and using $|e^{i\theta}|=1$ and $|e^{iaRe^{i\theta}}| = e^{-aR\sin\theta}$: \[\begin{aligned}\left|\int_{\mathcal{C}_R}f(z)\,dz\right| &\leq R\int^\pi_0 |g(Re^{i\theta})|\,e^{-aR\sin\theta}\,d\theta \notag\\ &\leq M(R)\cdot R\int^\pi_0 e^{-aR\sin\theta}\,d\theta,\end{aligned}\tag{1.4}\] where $M(R)\equiv\max_{\theta\in[0,\pi]}|g(Re^{i\theta})|$. By symmetry of $\sin\theta$ about $\theta=\pi/2$: \[R\int^\pi_0 e^{-aR\sin\theta}\,d\theta = 2R\int^{\pi/2}_0 e^{-aR\sin\theta}\,d\theta.\tag{1.5}\] Since $\sin\theta$ is concave on $[0,\pi/2]$, it lies above the chord connecting $(0,0)$ and $(\pi/2,1)$: \[\sin\theta \geq \frac{2\theta}{\pi}, \qquad \theta\in\left[0,\tfrac{\pi}{2}\right].\tag{1.6}\] Therefore: \[2R\int^{\pi/2}_0 e^{-aR\sin\theta}\,d\theta \leq 2R\int^{\pi/2}_0 e^{-2aR\theta/\pi}\,d\theta = \frac{\pi}{a}\!\left(1-e^{-aR}\right) \leq \frac{\pi}{a}.\tag{1.7}\] Combining, we obtain the bound \[\left|\int_{\mathcal{C}_R}f(z)\,dz\right| \leq \frac{\pi}{a}\,M(R). \tag{1.8}\] Taking $R\to\infty$: by assumption (1.1), $M(R)\to 0$, so the right-hand side of (1.8) vanishes by the squeeze theorem, and hence \[\lim_{R\to\infty}\int_{\mathcal{C}_R}f(z)\,d z = 0.\tag{1.9}\]
