11-Dimensional Supergravity

M-theory is a quantum theory of gravity in $d=11$ spacetime dimensions. It arises from the strong coupling limit of string theory. At low-energies, it is described by $d=11$ supergravity whose bosonic fields are the metric and a 4-form $G = dC$ where $C$ is a 3-form potential. The action governing these fields is \[S = \frac{1}{2}M_{\mathrm{pl}}^9\left[\int d^{11}x\sqrt{-g} \left(R - \frac{1}{48}G_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}G^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\right) - \frac{1}{6}\int C\wedge G\wedge G\right]\tag{1}\]

  1. Show that, up to surface terms, this action is gauge invariant under $C\to C + d\Lambda$ where $\Lambda$ is a 2-form.
    Solution.

    Under such a transformation, the 4-form $G \to G$, because of $d^2 = 0$. Therefore, the first integral in the action remains unchanged after the gauge transformation.

    The second integral requires a bit more treatment. Under the transformation, the integral becomes \[\int_M C \wedge G\wedge G \to \int_M C \wedge G\wedge G + \int_M d\Lambda \wedge G\wedge G.\tag{2}\] Now, using the Stoke's theorem, we find \[\int_M d\Lambda \wedge G\wedge G = \int_{\partial M} \Lambda \wedge G\wedge G-\int_M \Lambda\wedge d(G\wedge G) = 0,\tag{3}\] where we have used the fact that the fields vanish at the boundary $\partial M$ and $dG = d^2C =0$.

  2. Vary the metric to determine the Einstein equation for this theory.
    Solution.

    Let us consider the variation term by term. The first term gives the standard \[\delta(\sqrt{-g}R) = \sqrt{-g}G_{\mu\nu}\delta g^{\mu\nu}.\tag{4}\] The second term gives \[\frac{1}{96}\sqrt{-g}g_{\mu\nu}G_{\alpha\beta\rho\sigma}G^{\alpha\beta\rho\sigma}\delta g^{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{12}G_{\mu\beta\rho\sigma}\tensor{G}{_\nu^{\beta\rho\sigma}}\delta g^{\mu\nu}.\tag{5}\] The last integral is a pure topological term, and has no metric dependence.

    Therefore, we find the Einstein equation to be \[G_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{96}\left(8G_{\mu\beta\rho\sigma}\tensor{G}{_\nu^{\beta\rho\sigma}} - g_{\mu\nu}G_{\alpha\beta\rho\sigma}G^{\alpha\beta\rho\sigma}\right).\tag{6}\]

  3. Vary $C$ to obtain the equation of motion for the 4-form, \[d\star G = \frac{1}{2}G\wedge G.\tag{7}\]
    Solution.

    Now, we vary the 3-form, $C\to C + \delta C$. Note that the $GG$ term can be written as \[\int d^{11}x\sqrt{-g}G_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}G^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}=-4!\int G\wedge \star G.\tag{8}\] Therefore, the variation is given by \[-48\int_M d\delta C \wedge \star G = -48\int_{\partial M} \delta C \wedge \star G + 48\int_M\delta C\wedge d \star G.\tag{9}\] Again, the boundary term vanishes.

    The $CGG$ term gives \[\int\delta C\wedge G \wedge G + \int C\wedge \delta G \wedge G + \int C\wedge G \wedge \delta G.\tag{10}\] Note that \[\int C\wedge \delta G \wedge G = -\int d(C\wedge \delta C \wedge G)\ = \int d(C\wedge G)\ \wedge \delta C = \int G\wedge G \wedge \delta C,\tag{11}\] where we have neglected the boundary terms implicitly. Therefore, the $CGG$ term becomes \[3\int G\wedge G \wedge \delta C.\tag{12}\] putting the two terms together and set the variation to zero, we find \[\frac{3}{6}\int G\wedge G \wedge \delta C -\frac{48}{48}\int_M d \star G\wedge\delta C = 0 \implies d \star G = \frac{1}{2}G\wedge G.\tag{13}\]
