Green's Function for a Second-Order ODE with Heaviside Forcing

Source: Imperial College London, Carlo Contaldi, Differential Equations, Problem Sheet 9, Question 4.

Consider the following inhomogeneous ODE for $y(x)$: \[\hat{L}[y] = y'' + 3y' + 2y = \Theta(x - x_0) \qquad (0 \leq x \leq 1), \tag{1.1}\] with homogeneous BCs $y(0) = y(1) = 0$, where $0 \leq x \leq 1$ and $\Theta(x)$ is the Heaviside (unit) step function.

  1. Show that the Green's function for solving the above problem is \[G(x,z) = \frac{e^{z-2x}}{e-1} \begin{cases} (e^x - e^0)(e^z - e^1) & x < z \\[4pt] (e^x - e^1)(e^z - e^0) & x > z \end{cases}\tag{1.2}\]
  2. Show that this Green's function is not symmetrical.
  3. Use the Green's function to find the solution to the above inhomogeneous problem (i.e.\ formed from the inhomogeneous ODE with the specified BCs), and show that it is \[y(x) = \frac{e^{-2x}}{2(e-1)} \left[(e-1)(e^x - e^{x_0})^2\Theta(x-x_0) - (e^x - 1)(e - e^{x_0})^2\right].\tag{1.3}\] Hint: Consider the cases $x < x_0$ and $x > x_0$ separately.

The homogeneous equation $y''+3y'+2y=0$ holds everywhere except $x=z$, with characteristic equation \[\lambda^2+3\lambda+2=0 \implies \lambda=-2,-1.\tag{1.4}\] The Green's function therefore takes the piecewise form \[G(x,z)=\begin{cases} A(z)e^{-2x}+B(z)e^{-x} & 0\leq x<z\\[4pt] C(z)e^{-2x}+D(z)e^{-x} & z<x\leq 1. \end{cases}\tag{1.5}\] Applying the homogeneous BCs $G(0,z)=G(1,z)=0$: \[A+B=0, \qquad Ce^{-2}+De^{-1}=0 \implies B=-A, \quad C=-De.\tag{1.6}\] Continuity of $G$ at $x=z$ (substituting $B=-A$, $C=-De$): \[Ae^{-2z}-Ae^{-z}=-Dee^{-2z}+De^{-z} \implies A = D\,\frac{e^{-z}-e\,e^{-2z}}{e^{-2z}-e^{-z}}. \tag{1.7}\] The jump condition $[\partial_x G]_{x=z^-}^{x=z^+}=1$ (coefficient of $y''$ is unity) gives: \[\underbrace{\left(2De\,e^{-2z}-De^{-z}\right)}_{G'(z^+)} -\underbrace{\left(-2Ae^{-2z}+Ae^{-z}\right)}_{G'(z^-)}=1. \tag{1.8}\] Substituting (1.7) into (1.8) and expanding the denominator: \[\begin{aligned}&2e\,e^{-4z}-e^{-3z}-2e\,e^{-3z}+e^{-2z} +2e^{-3z}-2e\,e^{-4z}-e^{-2z}+e\,e^{-3z}\notag\\ &\quad =e^{-3z}-e\,e^{-3z}=e^{-3z}(1-e).\end{aligned}\tag{1.9}\] Hence: \[D = \frac{e^{-2z}-e^{-z}}{e^{-3z}(1-e)} = e^z\,\frac{e^0-e^z}{1-e} = \frac{e^z(1-e^z)}{1-e},\tag{1.10}\] and from (1.7): \[A = \frac{e^z}{1-e}(e^z-e).\tag{1.11}\] Assembling $G$ for $x<z$ using $B=-A$: \[\begin{aligned}G(x,z) &= A(e^{-2x}-e^{-x}) = \frac{e^z(e^z-e)}{1-e}(e^{-2x}-e^{-x})\notag\\ &= \frac{e^{z-2x}}{1-e}(e^z-e)(1-e^x) = \frac{e^{z-2x}}{e-1}(e^x-e^0)(e^z-e^1).\end{aligned}\tag{1.12}\] Similarly, for $x>z$: \[G(x,z) = \frac{e^{z-2x}}{e-1}(e^x-e^1)(e^z-e^0).\tag{1.13}\] Combining them, we find \[\boxed{G(x,z)=\frac{e^{z-2x}}{e-1}\begin{cases} (e^x-e^0)(e^z-e^1) & x<z\\[4pt] (e^x-e^1)(e^z-e^0) & x>z. \end{cases}}\tag{1.14}\]

It is rather clear that the Green's function we obtained does not have symmetry. This is expected, since the differential operator is not self-adjoint. To see this, consider the two equations: \[\hat{L}_x G(x,z) = \delta(x-z), \qquad \hat{L}_x G(x,z') = \delta(x-z').\tag{1.15}\] Multiplying the first by $G(x,z')$, the second by $G(x,z)$, subtracting and integrating: \[\int_a^b \left[G(x,z')\hat{L}_x G(x,z) - G(x,z)\hat{L}_x G(x,z')\right]dx = G(z',z) - G(z,z'). \tag{1.16}\] The left-hand side can be rewritten as: \[\int_a^b G(x,z')\left[\hat{L}_x - \hat{L}_x^\dagger\right]G(x,z)\,\dd x + \text{boundary terms}.\tag{1.17}\] If $\hat{L}$ is self-adjoint ($\hat{L} = \hat{L}^\dagger$) and the boundary terms vanish under homogeneous BCs, the left-hand side of (1.16) is zero, giving $G(z',z)=G(z,z')$.

For the operator $\hat{L} = \partial_x^2 + 3\partial_x + 2$, integrating by parts shows: \[\hat{L}^\dagger = \partial_x^2 - 3\partial_x + 2 \neq \hat{L},\tag{1.18}\] since the odd-derivative term changes sign. Hence: \[\hat{L} - \hat{L}^\dagger = 6\partial_x \neq 0,\tag{1.19}\] and the left-hand side of (1.16) does not vanish.

Now, we are ready to obtain the particular solution to the differential equation: \[y(x) = \int_0^1dzG(x,z)\Theta(z-x_0).\tag{1.20}\]

For $x<x_0$, the discontinuity of the Green's function is to the left of $x_0$, and thus the integral becomes \[\begin{aligned}y(x<x_0) &= \int_{x_0}^1dz\,G_{x<z}(x,z)= \frac{e^{-2x}(e^x-1)}{e-1}\left(-\frac{1}{2}e^2-\frac{1}{2}e^{2x_0}+ee^{x_0}\right) \notag\\ &=-\frac{e^{-2x}}{2(e-1)}(e^x-1)(e^{x_0}-e)^2.\end{aligned}\tag{1.21}\]

For $x>x_0$, the integral becomes \[\begin{aligned}y(x>x_0) &= \int_{x_0}^{x}dz\,G_{x>z}(x,z) + \int_{x}^1dz\,G_{x<z}(x,z) \notag\\ &= y(x\to x_0^-) + \frac{e^{-2x}(e^x-e)}{e-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}e^{2x_0}-e^{x_0}- \frac{1}{2}e^{2x} + e^x\right).\end{aligned}\tag{1.22}\]

Putting them together, we find \[\boxed{y(x) = \frac{e^{-2x}}{2(e-1)} \left[(e-1)(e^x - e^{x_0})^2\Theta(x-x_0) - (e^x - 1)(e - e^{x_0})^2\right].}\tag{1.23}\]
