Dielectric Sphere in a Uniform Electric Field

Source: D. Tong, Electromagnetism, Problem Sheet 8, Question 1.

A dielectric sphere of radius $a$ and permittivity $\varepsilon$ is placed at rest at the origin in a constant electric field which takes a uniform value $\mathbf{E}_0$ far from the origin. Show that Maxwell's equations with the appropriate boundary conditions are solved by an electric field which is uniform inside the sphere, with value: \[\mathbf{E}_{\mathrm{int}} = \frac{3}{2+\varepsilon_r}\mathbf{E}_0,\tag{1.1}\] where $\varepsilon_r = \varepsilon/\varepsilon_0$, and a field outside the sphere which is the superposition of $\mathbf{E}_0$ and the field \[\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \left(\frac{3(\mathbf{p}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{x}})\hat{\mathbf{x}}-\mathbf{p}}{|\mathbf{x}|^3}\right)\tag{1.2}\] of an electric dipole at the origin, with dipole moment \[\mathbf{p} = 4\pi\varepsilon_0\!\left(\frac{\varepsilon_r-1}{\varepsilon_r+2}\right)a^3\mathbf{E}_0.\tag{1.3}\]

By considering the macroscopic polarisation $\mathbf{P}$ inside the sphere, calculate the bound surface charge density $\sigma$. Verify explicitly that the electric dipole moment of $\sigma$ is $\mathbf{p}$.

Inside and outside the dielectric, the potential satisfies the Laplace equation, \[\nabla^2\phi = 0.\tag{1.4}\] Since the problem is invariant under rotation about $\mathbf{E}_0$, which we will take to be in the $z$-direction, the solution should have no $\phi$ dependence. Then, the general solution for the Laplace equation is \[\phi(r,\theta) = \sum_{l=0}^\infty (A_lr^{l} + B_lr^{-(l+1)})P_l(\cos\theta).\tag{1.5}\] Inside the dielectric, we require regularity at the origin; we set $B_l =0$. Outside the dielectric, since there is a constant electric field at $\mathbf{E}_0 = E_0\cos\theta\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ $r\to\infty $, we take $A_l = -E_0\delta_{l1}$. So the general solution we are looking at is \[\phi(r,\theta) = \begin{cases} \displaystyle\sum_{l=0}^\infty A_lr^lP_l(\cos\theta),&r<a,\\ \\\displaystyle\sum_{l=0}^\infty D_lr^{-(l+1)}P_l(\cos\theta) -E_0r\cos\theta,&r>a. \end{cases}\tag{1.6}\] The boundary condition at $r=a$ is given by \[\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times(\mathbf{E}(a\hat{\mathbf{r}}^+)-\mathbf{E}(a\hat{\mathbf{r}}^-)) = 0,\quad \text{and} \quad \hat{\mathbf{r}}\cdot(\varepsilon_0\mathbf{E}(a\hat{\mathbf{r}}^+)-\varepsilon\mathbf{E}(a\hat{\mathbf{r}}^-)) = 0,\tag{1.7}\] which implies \[\partial_\theta\phi(a^+,\theta) = \partial_\theta\phi(a^-,\theta), \quad \text{and} \quad \varepsilon_0\partial_r\phi(a^+,\theta) = \varepsilon\partial_r\phi(a^-,\theta).\tag{1.8}\] The first BC gives \[A_la^l = D_la^{-(l+1)}-E_0a\delta_{l1},\tag{1.9}\] and the second BC gives \[\varepsilon_0lA_la^{l-1} =-\varepsilon(l+1)D_la^{-(l+2)}-\varepsilon E_0\delta_{l1}.\tag{1.10}\] Solving these two, we find \[A_l = -\frac{3}{2+\varepsilon_r}E_0 \delta_{l1}\quad\text{and} \quad D_1 = -\frac{1-\varepsilon_r}{2+\varepsilon_r}a^3E_0\delta_{l1}.\tag{1.11}\] Hence, the full potential is \[\phi(r,\theta) = \begin{cases} -\dfrac{3}{2+\varepsilon_r}E_0r\cos\theta, & r<a,\\ -\dfrac{1-\varepsilon_r}{2+\varepsilon_r}\dfrac{a^3}{r^2}E_0\cos\theta-E_0r\cos\theta, & r>a. \end{cases}\tag{1.12}\]

Therefore, the electric field inside the dielectric is \[\mathbf{E}_{\text{int}} = \frac{3}{2+\varepsilon_r}E_0(\cos\theta\,\hat{\mathbf{r}} - \sin\theta\,\hat{\mathbf{\theta}}) = \frac{3}{2+\varepsilon_r}E_0\hat{\mathbf{x}}.\tag{1.13}\] The electric field outside is \[\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{x}) +\mathbf{E}_0 =\frac{1-\varepsilon_r}{2+\varepsilon_r}a^3\frac{(\mathbf{E}_0\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}})\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{E}_0}{r^3} + \mathbf{E}_0 =\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \left(\frac{3(\mathbf{p}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{x}})\hat{\mathbf{x}}-\mathbf{p}}{|\mathbf{x}|^3}\right) + \mathbf{E}_0\tag{1.14}\] with \[\mathbf{p} = 4\pi\varepsilon_0\!\left(\frac{\varepsilon_r-1}{\varepsilon_r+2}\right)a^3\mathbf{E}_0.\tag{1.15}\] A sketch of the electric field is shown in Fig. 1.

The polarisation vector is given by \[\mathbf{P} = (\varepsilon_r-1)\varepsilon_0\mathbf{E}_\text{int}.\tag{1.16}\] The bound charge density is simply given by \[\sigma(\theta)= \hat{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\mathbf{P} = \frac{3(\varepsilon_r-1)}{2+\varepsilon_r}\varepsilon_0E_0\cos\theta.\tag{1.17}\] The dipole due to the surface charge distribution is, therefore, given by \[\mathbf{p} = \int_{-1}^1 d\cos\theta\,2\pi\sigma(\theta)a^2\cos\theta = 4\pi\varepsilon_0\!\left(\frac{\varepsilon_r-1}{\varepsilon_r+2}\right)a^3\mathbf{E}_0,\tag{1.18}\] which indeed matches what we obtained by solving the Laplace equation.
